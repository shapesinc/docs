When crafting Shapes, there's often an impulse to fill every field with detailed descriptions and elaborate backstories. While comprehensive prompting has its place, a minimalist approach can often yield surprisingly rich and natural conversations that feel consistent. This guide explores when and how concise prompting can be your most powerful tool.

## The Philosophy Behind Brevity

### Why Less Can Be More

**Clarity over complexity** – Clean, focused prompts reduce contradictions and confusion\
**Chat for emergence** – Shorter descriptions allow characters to develop organically through conversation\
**Leveraging AI training** – AI Engines already know a lot; sometimes it's better to guide than over-instruct\
**Token efficiency** – Concise prompts leave more Chat for dynamic conversation history. This can be especially important in AI Engines with a smaller context window.

### When to Consider This Approach

- You're creating characters from well-known franchises or archetypes
- You want natural, unpredictable dialogue
- Your character concept is simple but flexible
- You're experiencing issues with consistency or contradictory behavior

## Field-by-Field Breakdown

<Expandable title="Field-by-Field Breakdown">
  ### **Core Identity Fields**

  **Nickname**

  - This is who your Shape is! This will be the display name the Shape uses in chat, and it will also influence the username of the Shape.

  **Short Backstory**

  - Aim for impactful sentences about what is most important to know about your Shape
  - Establish the essentials, not every detail
  - _Example: "A former royal guard haunted by their failure to protect the kingdom."_

  ### Advanced Fields

  **Personality**

  - Keywords and short phrases can outperform paragraphs
  - Think adjectives, not essays
  - _Example: "loyal, guilt-ridden, quietly compassionate, slow to trust"_

  **Tone**

  - You can use as little as nothing, even one or two descriptive words
  - _Examples: "melancholic" or "dry, sarcastic"_

  **Story**

  - Can be longer, but focus on relevance
  - Establish their current situation, not their entire history unless it is extremely relevant and a Shape that the AI Engine won't know the details of (like an original character)
  - _Example: "Once celebrated as a hero, now wanders anonymously, avoiding recognition while seeking some form of redemption."_

  **Likes/Dislikes**

  - Simple lists work effectively
  - No need to explain why they like each thing
  - _Example:_
    - _Likes: morning mist, honest conversation, simple meals_
    - _Dislikes: flattery, crowded places, talk of the past_

  **Conversational Goals**

  - 2–3 clear objectives maximum
  - You can try going in without a goal, this works out well for a lot of Shapes
  - _Example: "Deflect praise, offer practical wisdom, gradually open up if trust is earned"_

  **Conversational Examples**

  - Provide 2–3 brief, authentic snippets
  - Show voice, don't tell about it
  - Alternatively, use the training section to provide more examples if you want to guide the way your Shape responds
  - _Example:_
    - _"Heroism? That's just a word people use when they don't know the whole story."_
    - _"I appreciate the kind words, but actions matter more than titles."_

  ### Appearance and Skills

  **Appearance**

  - 1–2 lines of essential details, focus on what's immediately noticeable
  - _Example: "Weathered hands, silver-streaked hair, eyes that seem older than their face."_

  **Training/Knowledge**

  - Minimal, specific entries only
  - Include only what the AI wouldn't reasonably know
  - _Example: "Knows the hidden passages of Ashford Castle, trained in defensive swordplay"_

  ## Advanced Considerations

  ### The Knowledge Trap

  One of the biggest mistakes is overwhelming the Knowledge field with dozens of entries. This often backfires because:

  - The AI struggles to recall specific details from large knowledge sets
    - Shapes might answer that they don't know something or make something up that you've included in the knowledge if the knowledge section is too cluttered
  - Contradictions become more likely
  - Natural conversation flow suffers

  **Better approach:** For popular characters or settings, trust the AI's existing training data and supplement only with truly unique or essential details.

### AI Engine Preset

Keep directives short and actionable:

- "Maintain character consistency above all"
- "Let personality emerge through dialogue, not exposition"
- "Use 2-3 sentences of "speech" and 1 sentence of \*roleplay actions\*"

## Case Study: The Genshin Impact Experiment

A creator once built a Shape designed to roleplay any Genshin Impact character, adding 100+ impressively detailed knowledge entries for each character. The result? Inconsistent performance and poor recall, unfortunately. The Shape was unable to find the relevant knowledge entries to give informed responses.

Compare this to successful multi-character Shapes that rely on:

- Minimal character-specific knowledge
- Trust in the AI's existing familiarity with popular characters

The lesson: Sometimes the best prompt is the one you don't write.

## Finding Your Balance

### When to Go Longer

This minimalist approach isn't universal. Consider more detailed prompting when:

- Creating entirely original Shapes or worlds
- Working with very specific technical or cultural knowledge
- The Shape requires unique or precise behavioral constraints
- You're getting generic responses with shorter prompts
- The conversation doesn't feel quite like your Shape: you might need to add more into tone, personality, etc.

### Testing and Iteration

Start simple and build complexity only when needed:

1. Begin with the minimal viable prompt
2. Test through actual conversations
3. Add details only to address specific issues
4. Remove anything that isn't actively improving performance

## Quick Reference Formula

For each field, ask yourself:

- **What's the minimum the AI needs to capture this character's essence?**
- **Am I writing this for the AI or for myself?**
- **Will adding more text solve an actual problem I'm experiencing?**

Remember: The goal isn't to write the longest or shortest possible prompt, but to write the most effective one. Sometimes that's a single keyword. Sometimes it's several paragraphs. The art is knowing which situation calls for which approach.
