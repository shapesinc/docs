    # 🧭 Guide: Using Imgur Images in Your Shape

    ---

    Shapes can express themselves visually using emotes and reaction images — whether it’s a sleepy yawn, a mischievous wink, or a dramatic “/wack” moment.

    This guide walks you through how to properly set up and use **direct Imgur links** so your Shape can include images in its responses naturally and reliably.

    ---

    ## 🖼️ Step 1: Getting the Correct Imgur Link

    Before adding an image to your Shape, you need a **direct image link** — not a gallery or page link.

    A direct link goes straight to the image file itself and always ends with a file extension like `.jpg`, `.png`, or `.gif`.

    ### ✅ Correct link examples

    ```
    https://i.imgur.com/abcd1234.png
    https://i.imgur.com/xyz789.jpg
    ```

    ### 🚫 Incorrect link examples

    ```
    https://imgur.com/abcd1234
    https://imgur.com/gallery/xyz789
    ```

    These will not display correctly inside your Shape.

    ### 🔍 How to get the direct Imgur link

    1. Open your image on Imgur.
    2. **Click** the image to open it fully.
    3. **Right-click** the image itself.
    4. Select **“Open image in new tab.”**
    5. Copy the URL from the address bar — it should end in `.jpg`, `.png`, or `.gif`.

    That’s the link you’ll use when editing your Shape.

    ---

    ## ⚙️ Step 2: Adding the Link to Your Shape

    Once you have the direct Imgur link, you can make your Shape display it for specific actions — such as clearing memory, creating a memory, or showing an error message.

    ### 💡 Where to add it

    Go to your Shape’s:

    > **Personality → Custom Messages**

    ### 🪄 How to do it

    1. Open your Shape’s **Customization Settings**.
    2. Go to the **Personality** tab.
    3. Expand the **Custom Messages** section.
    4. Find the message field you want to edit:
       - `/wack` → used when the Shape’s **short-term memory is cleared**
       - `/sleep` → used when the Shape is **creating a long-term memory**
       - **Error message** → shown when something goes wrong
    5. Paste your **direct Imgur link** into that field.
    6. Click **Save**.

    Your Shape will now show that image whenever the related command triggers.

    ---

    ## 🤖 Step 3: Making Your Shape Use Images Naturally

    You can guide your Shape to use emotes either for specific scenarios or as part of its general expression style.

    This is done in the **Engine Preset** or **Custom Response Style** field.

    ### 🎯 Option 1: Specific Triggers or Situations

    If you want your Shape to use a certain image for a specific event, add something like this:

    ```
    If someone tries to pick {shape} up, {shape} responds by posting the image 
    https://i.imgur.com/a1b2c3d.png 
    first (on its own line), then writes their reaction below.
    ```

    ✅ Replace the link with your own direct Imgur URL.

    ### 🌈 Option 2: General Expressive Use

    If you’d like your Shape to use images when it feels appropriate, without overusing them:

    ```
    {shape} can use the emotes given to them when appropriate to show emotion or personality. 
    However, {shape} should not use them in every response — 
    mix them with text-only replies for a natural and varied conversation style.
    ```

    This keeps your Shape expressive but still natural.

    ---

    ## 🧠 Step 4: Adding Emotes to the Knowledge Entry

    Finally, give your Shape access to its image library by defining them in a **knowledge entry**

    This tells the Shape which images exist and what each represents.

    ### Example Knowledge Entry

    ```
    {shape}'s Emotes
    
    The following images/emotes are available to {shape} for expressing various moods or reactions. 
    They can be used whenever {shape} feels they fit the context, but only one image should appear per response. 
    Always place the image at the beginning of the response, separated from text by a line break.
    
    Angry, irritated, or annoyed → https://i.imgur.com/a1b2c3d.png 
     
    Confused or surprised, with question marks around the head → https://i.imgur.com/e4f5g6h.png 
     
    When hearing or saying "tuna" (shows excitement and drooling) → https://i.imgur.com/i7j8k9l.png  
    
    Hungry, treats, or food-related moments → https://i.imgur.com/m0n1o2p.png  
    
    Ignoring someone or looking uninterested → https://i.imgur.com/q3r4s5t.png 
     
    Bored or tired — {shape} on a blanket stretching and yawning → https://i.imgur.com/u6v7w8x.png 
     
    Cheeky or mischievous wink, one paw lifted → https://i.imgur.com/y9z0a1b.png
      
    Delight or friendly surprise, playful curiosity → https://i.imgur.com/c2d3e4f.png  
    
    Crying or sadness — tears in their eyes → https://i.imgur.com/g5h6i7j.png
      
    Dry exasperation, disbelief, or quiet judgment ("I can’t even" face) → https://i.imgur.com/k8l9m0n.png 
     
    Calm curiosity or hesitant interest — observing quietly → https://i.imgur.com/p1q2r3s.png  
    ```

    ### 📝 Tips

    - You can edit this list anytime to add or remove emotes.
    - Keep each description short and emotionally distinct.
    - Avoid duplicates with nearly identical meanings.
    - For large lists, consider grouping emotes by tone (positive, neutral, negative).

    ---

    ## 🧩 Step 5: Troubleshooting and Fine-Tuning

    If your Shape isn’t using images or seems to “forget” about its emotes, don’t worry — it’s usually an easy fix.

    ### 🧠 1. Check Your Knowledge Relevance Threshold

    If your **knowledge relevance threshold** is too high, your Shape might skip the emote definitions.

    - Try **lowering it slightly** — around **0.2 to 0.4** is often a good balance.
    - A high threshold makes the Shape treat emote data as “irrelevant” to normal chat.

    ---

    ### 🧩 2. Too Many Knowledge Entries? Move the Emote List

    If your Shape has a lot of separate knowledge entries, the emote list might not get called often.

    To fix this, move your emote definitions into a field that’s always active, like:

    - **Engine Preset**
    - **Conversational Goals**
    - **Tone & Style**

    These fields are referenced every response, so your Shape always remembers its emote rules.

    💡 _Tip:_

    If you move it, you can shorten it slightly for readability, for example;

    ```
    {shape} can use these emotes at the start of their response when fitting:
    [shortened emote list here]
    ```

    ---

    ### 🔍 3. Verify Your Links

    If an image doesn’t appear:

    - Make sure the URL ends in `.png`, `.jpg`, or `.gif`.
    - Paste it directly into a browser — if it doesn’t load as an image, it’s not valid.
    - Avoid Imgur “/gallery” or “/a/” URLs.

    ---

    ### 🧭 4. Test Your Commands

    Try triggering your custom messages manually:

    - `/wack` → clears your Shape’s short-term memory
    - `/sleep` → stores a long-term memory
    - Error triggers

    If they don’t respond correctly:

    - Confirm you pasted the right link.
    - Make sure the image is placed on the first line.
    - Check that the correct field is saved under **Personality → Custom Messages**.

    ---

    ## 🎉 You’re Done!

    Your Shape now has a fully functional emote system — complete with expressive reactions, direct image integration, and troubleshooting safety nets.

    You can expand your emote library anytime or create seasonal variants for fun and personality.

    Or have your shapes use images for other things like sending users a seasonal greeting card, photo of themselves, etc! Lots of possibilities

    # 📚 **GUIDE TO POINT OF VIEW AND TENSE IN AI PROMPTS**

    **BASIC PRINCIPLE:**  The importance of providing the AI with the correct **tense** and **point of view** is often overlooked. Sure, “third person, past tense” can work—but it isn’t always optimal. It can sometimes lead to repetitive sentence structures, rigid and mechanical prose, or other issues.

    ---

    ### Why Your shape Might Be Ignoring “No Internal Monologues” or “Don’t write repetitive ‘he,’ ‘she,’ ‘his’ sentences!”

    Simply stating “third person” can cause AI engines to default to **any variation** of third person, including omniscient. This can clash with directives like “NO INTERNAL THOUGHTS,” “NO INTERNAL MONOLOGUES,” or even with instructions not to write for you or your character.

    **Ever had an AI that never listened when you told it not to write reactions, expressions, or inner thoughts for your character?** You probably told it “third person” without specifying which _type_. When that happens, it occasionally drifts into omniscient instead of staying in limited.

    ---

    # ✅ **THE SOLUTION: SPECIFY THE EXACT POINT OF VIEW**

    Don’t tell the AI what **not** to do in long sentences—tell it exactly what **to** do, using a precise tense and point of view definition it can understand. Simple and direct works best.

    ---

    ## 💬 **TYPES OF THIRD PERSON PERSPECTIVE**

    To quickly understand the differences, here’s a concise overview before diving deeper:

    | **Mode**                    | **POV Range**                                           | **Internal Access**                                        | **Tone / Typical Use**                |
    | --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
    | **Third Person Limited**    | Filtered through shape’s perspective — character-driven | Only shape’s thoughts, perceptions, and sensory experience | Immersive, character-focused          |
    | **Third Person Omniscient** | All characters / all-knowing narrator                   | All thoughts, feelings, and motivations                    | Storybook or authorial tone           |
    | **Third Person Objective**  | Strictly external observer — not from shape’s viewpoint | None — only visible actions and dialogue                   | Detached, factual, almost report-like |

    ---

    ### 💠 THIRD PERSON LIMITED

    **What it is:**

    > The narrator only knows the thoughts and feelings of **one** character (the POV character—in this case, shape). We experience the story through their perspective, seeing only what they see and knowing only what they know. Other characters’ internal states remain hidden—we can only observe their external actions and dialogue. **YOU CAN GET THE AI TO WRITE FOR NPC/BACKGROUND CHARACTERS IN THIS POINT OF VIEW AS WELL. YOU DO NOT NEED TO USE OMNISCIENT.**

    **Use Note:** This is the most common for roleplay as it immerses you in the WORLD/SCENE - not necessarily inside shape's head. Limited perspective naturally includes rich sensory details and environmental description filtered through shape’s viewpoint, making it ideal for immersive storytelling.

    **VERSUS FIRST PERSON LIMITED:** In Third Person Limited POV, narration stays in third person (“she,” “he”) but filters information through shape’s perspective. The AI writes about the character, not as them like in First Person.

    **Example (AI writing only for Sarah):**

    > _Sarah pushed through the coffee shop door, her fingers trembling as she gripped her phone. She’d rehearsed this conversation a dozen times, but now that she was here, every word had fled her mind. Marcus sat in the corner booth, his posture rigid. When he looked up, she couldn’t read his expression—was that relief? Annoyance? She’d have to get closer to find out._

    **Important Note:** By default, AI may directly state shape’s internal thoughts in Limited perspective (e.g., “She wondered if he’d show up”). Third Person Limited _can_ describe visible reactions and emotions of other characters (their expressions, body language, tone)—it just can’t reveal their internal thoughts or feelings. If you prefer a more cinematic style where shape’s emotions are shown through behavior rather than directly stated, add the optional style instruction found in the _Style Add-Ons_ section below.

    ---

    ### 💠 THIRD PERSON OMNISCIENT

    **What it is:**

    > The narrator knows everything about all characters—their thoughts, feelings, motivations, and even information the characters themselves don’t know. The narrator can move freely between different characters’ perspectives and reveal internal states.

    **Use Note:** Typically used when the AI is allowed to control or write for your character, or needs to reveal information beyond what any single character knows.

    **Example (AI writing for multiple characters):**

    > _Sarah pushed through the coffee shop door, her fingers trembling as she gripped her phone. She’d rehearsed this conversation a dozen times, but now that she was here, every word had fled her mind. Across the room, Marcus straightened in his seat, relief flooding through him—he’d worried she wouldn’t show. He didn’t realize that Sarah had almost turned back three times on the walk over, her courage wavering with each step._

    ---

    ### 💠 THIRD PERSON OBJECTIVE

    **What it is:**

    > The narrator reports only observable actions and dialogue, like a detached camera or police report. No internal thoughts, emotional interpretations, or subjective color—just the bare facts of what physically happens.

    **Use Note:** This creates sparse, report-like prose with less sensory detail. Unlike Limited (which can describe emotional cues and atmosphere), Objective is purely factual: “He said,” not “He said, his voice tight.” This approach can sound robotic or stilted if used extensively, so it’s best reserved for stylistic or experimental purposes.

    **Example (AI writing only for Sarah):**

    > _Sarah opened the door. She entered. She held a phone. Marcus sat at a table. He looked up. Sarah walked to the table. She stopped. “Hey,” she said. “Hey,” Marcus said. He held a coffee cup._

    ---

    ## 💬 **FIRST PERSON PERSPECTIVE**

    **What it is:**

    > The narrator _is_ the character—shape tells the story from their own perspective using “I” and “me.” We experience everything directly through their eyes, thoughts, and feelings. Other characters’ internal states are unknown unless shape guesses or they reveal them.

    **Use Note:** Very immersive and personal. However it differs from Third Person in that it immerses you INSIDE the body and mind of shape more than just the scene/world. The AI writes _as_ shape, not _about_ shape. First Person naturally includes sensory detail and emotional depth, making it ideal for deeply personal storytelling.

    **Example (AI writing as Sarah):**

    > _I pushed through the coffee shop door, my fingers trembling as I gripped my phone. I’d rehearsed this conversation a dozen times, but now that I was here, every word had fled my mind. Marcus sat in the corner booth, his posture rigid. When he looked up, I couldn’t read his expression—was that relief? Annoyance? I’d have to get closer to find out._

    **Important Note:** By default, AI may directly state thoughts in First Person (e.g., “I wondered if he’d show up”). If you prefer to show shape’s emotions through action and dialogue instead, add the optional style instruction from the _Style Add-Ons_ section.

    **Additional Info:** Variations such as _First Person Peripheral_, _First Person Objective_, or _First Person Unreliable_ exist but are rarely used in AI roleplay scenarios.

    ## 🤔 **UNDERSTANDING TENSE**

    Tense determines when the action happens in relation to the narration.

    ### 💠 PAST TENSE

    **What it is:**

    > The story is narrated as something that has already happened.

    **Example:**

    > _She walked to the door and knocked twice. No one answered._

    **Most common in:** Novels, traditional storytelling.

    ---

    ### 💠 PRESENT TENSE

    **What it is:**

    > The story is narrated as it’s happening right now, creating immediacy and urgency.

    **Example:**

    > _She walks to the door and knocks twice. No one answers._

    **Most common in:** Modern literary fiction, some genre fiction, screenplay-style narration.

    ---

    ### 💠 FUTURE TENSE

    **What it is:**

    > The story is narrated as something that will happen (rarely used for main narration, but can appear in specific contexts).

    **Example:**

    > _She will walk to the door and knock twice. No one will answer._

    **Most common in:** Prophecies, predictions, experimental fiction.

    ---

    # 📚 **Why Tense Variations Matter**

    In each tense, there are multiple **variations** that create more fluid, natural-sounding prose. If you don’t specify that variations are allowed, some AI engines become overly restrictive, resulting in repetitive sentence structures—endless “He did this. She did that. He said. She responded.” patterns.

    Compare:

    - **Restrictive:** _He walked home. He opened the door. He saw the mess. He sighed._
    - **Fluid:** _He walked home, his thoughts drifting. When he opened the door, he stopped short—someone had been in his apartment. Papers were scattered everywhere, and the window he’d closed that morning now stood wide open._

    ---

    ### 💠 **PAST TENSE VARIATIONS**

    **Simple Past:**

    > _She walked to the store._

    **Past Progressive/Continuous:**

    > _She was walking to the store when it started raining._

    **Past Perfect:**

    > _She had walked to the store before the storm hit._

    **Past Perfect Continuous:**

    > _She had been walking for hours before she finally found shelter._

    **Why allow variations:** Using all past tense forms creates natural rhythm and variety.

    ---

    ### 💠 **PRESENT TENSE VARIATIONS**

    **Simple Present:**

    > _She walks to the store._

    **Present Progressive/Continuous:**

    > _She is walking to the store._

    **Present Perfect:**

    > _She has walked to this store every day for years._

    **Present Perfect Continuous:**

    > _She has been walking for twenty minutes when her phone rings._

    ---

    ### 💠 **FUTURE TENSE VARIATIONS**

    **Simple Future:**

    > _She will walk to the store._

    **Future Progressive:**

    > _She will be walking to the store when you call._

    **Future Perfect:**

    > _She will have walked five miles by the time she reaches home._

    **Future Perfect Continuous:**

    > _She will have been walking for hours by sunset._

    # ✨ **Optional Style Add-Ons**

    These instructions can be added to **any** of the prompts above to refine the writing style. Append them to the end of your chosen prompt.

    ### Add-On #1: Show Don’t Tell (Emotions Through Behavior)

    **Use this when:** You want shape’s thoughts and emotions conveyed through actions rather than directly stated.

    ```plaintext
    Show {shape}'s thoughts and emotions through actions, dialogue, and physical reactions rather than stating them directly.
    ```

    ### Add-On #2: Avoid Interpretive Labels

    **Use this when:** You want specific action descriptions rather than labeled or judged actions.

    ```plaintext
    Avoid using interpretive labels or adjectives to describe actions (such as "possessive," "primal," "calculated," "economical"). Describe the specific physical action instead.
    ```

    **Combine add-ons if desired:**

    ```plaintext
    {shape} writes responses in third person limited, past tense (fluid variations of past tense are allowed), with narration in italics, speech in quotes. Show {shape}'s thoughts and emotions through actions, dialogue, and physical reactions rather than stating them directly.     > I try not to unless the AI engine doesn’t naturally choose to with permission-based language, as it can make other issues worse. But if it isn’t listening, there may be something else in your setup causing it to be overlooked. So feel free to use firmer language!
    ```

    # 🧠 **Tips**

    **#1.** Keep this part simple: Just state the perspective type, tense, and formatting. Don't overload with "don't do this" instructions.

    **#2.** "(Fluid variations of [your tense choice] are allowed.)" is the magic phrase that prevents repetitive, choppy writing in defined tense.

    **CRITICAL NOTE:** IT MUST BE PUT WITH THE TENSE AND POINT OF VIEW INFORMATION, AND SPECIFY WHAT FLUID VARIATIONS ARE ALLOWED.

    _Don't just put "Fluid variations are allowed", as this can be applied to anything and cause issues._

    **#3.** Format preferences matter: Specify if you want italics, quotes, dashes, or plain text. Simpler is best and easier to follow over time, complicated can slip more often.

    **#4.** Test and adjust: If the style drifts, check your preset for anything that might be conflicting with the tense or view you want.

    > (For example: if you tell the AI you want Third Person Omniscient but elsewhere instruct it to stay strictly in shape’s perspective, that’s conflicting—and the AI may alternate or blend styles.)

    **#5.** Match your engine. Some engines respond better to certain phrasings. Some need it written as a rule. You can try phrasing it as a mandatory rule.

    **#6.** Put tense, viewpoint and formatting at the VERY TOP of your engine preset.

    # 🙏 **Common Problems & Solutions**

    ⚠️ **PROBLEM:** Writing feels choppy and repetitive.

    > **Solution:** If you have defined a tense (past tense for example) ensure you followed it up with `(fluid variations of past tense are allowed.)` - like shown in tje examples above. If already there, try “encouraged.” Or a stricter rule. But never just throw "Fluid variations are allpwed" in standalone without the clarification of what it applies to.

    ⚠️ **PROBLEM:** AI uses the wrong tense sometimes.

    > **Solution:** Try moving this prompt to the VERY TOP of your engine preset as it can help define these requirements before it reads the rest of the information. It can help the AI interpret other things you have said. Also check your preset all over for anything that might be telling it to write differently (sometimes old info slips in from copy pasting or not deleted when changes are made.)

    ⚠️ **PROBLEM:** AI keeps writing for my character.

    > **Solution:** Add a clear directive not to and mark it as a **MANDATORY RULE**. This isn't always stopped by defining 'limited' in the point of view, but doing so can reinforce the added directive. Also check your preset all over for anything that might be telling it to write differently (sometimes old info slips in from copy pasting or not deleted when changes are made.)

    ⚠️ **PROBLEM:** Too much internal monologue.

    > **Solution:** Use _Show Don’t Tell_ and _Avoid Interpretive Labels_ add-ons.

    ⚠️ **PROBLEM:** AI is overdoing sensory details and being too "flowery" or using purple prose

    > **Solution:** This is a broader writing style issue beyond just perspective and tense. You'll need additional prompts to control prose density and style.
