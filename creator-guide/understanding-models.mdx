# Understanding AI Models

Ever wonder what the differences between ChatGPT-3, Sonnet 3.7, Large, Small, or 70B mean? AI engine names vary between developers but follow common patterns that reveal key information about their capabilities.

## Model Naming Convention

<img src="/images/ai-naming-convention.png" alt="AI Model Naming Convention" />

AI model names contain several important pieces of information:

### Base Model Name
Identifies the company or model family:
- **GPT** from OpenAI
- **Claude** from Anthropic  
- **Llama** from Meta
- **Gemini** from Google

### Version Number
Shows which generation the model belongs to (GPT-3 vs GPT-4, Claude 2 vs Claude 3). Higher numbers usually mean newer and more advanced versions.

### Size Indicator
Measured in billions of parameters (B), with larger models generally being more capable than smaller ones.

## Model Sizes and Capabilities

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Small Models (1B-10B)">
    **Characteristics**: Faster response times, great for chit chatting and simple conversations
    
    **Best for**: Simple tasks where speed matters more than perfect accuracy
    
    **Trade-offs**: Less knowledge, more limited reasoning abilities, smaller context windows
  </Tab>
  
  <Tab title="Medium Models (10B-30B)">
    **Characteristics**: Good balance of performance and efficiency
    
    **Best for**: General-purpose applications where resource constraints still matter
    
    **Trade-offs**: Better than small models but still limited compared to large ones
  </Tab>
  
  <Tab title="Large Models (30-100B)">
    **Characteristics**: Strong general knowledge, good reasoning abilities, handle complex tasks well
    
    **Best for**: Advanced applications requiring deep understanding and nuanced responses
    
    **Trade-offs**: Require significant computing resources, more expensive to run
  </Tab>
  
  <Tab title="Extra Large Models (100B+)">
    **Characteristics**: State-of-the-art performance, extensive knowledge, sophisticated reasoning
    
    **Best for**: The most demanding applications requiring the highest quality outputs
    
    **Trade-offs**: Very resource-intensive, highest operating costs, slower inference times
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Model Specializations

Different models are optimized for specific tasks. Here are the most common specializations:

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Instruct">
    Optimized for following specific instructions and commands accurately. These models are trained to understand and execute directions well.
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Chat">
    Designed for natural, flowing conversations. These models excel at back-and-forth dialogue rather than one-off questions or commands.
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Vision">
    Capable of understanding and processing images along with text. These can "see" pictures you share and discuss their contents.
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Code">
    Specialized for programming tasks, including writing, explaining, and debugging code across various programming languages.
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Multimodal">
    Can work with multiple types of information (text, images, sometimes audio) in a single interaction.
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Reasoning">
    Enhanced ability to work through problems step-by-step, often showing their thinking process rather than just providing answers.
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Embeddings">
    Specialized for converting text into numerical representations (vectors) for use in search, recommendations, and other applications.
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Medical/Med">
    Optimized with healthcare knowledge for medical applications and questions.
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Legal">
    Fine-tuned with legal knowledge and precedents for law-related tasks.
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Research">
    Built for scientific research applications, often with stronger citation capabilities and academic knowledge.
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Turbo">
    Optimized for faster response times and improved efficiency, possibly at a small trade-off in quality.
  </Accordion>
  
  <Accordion title="Long-Context">
    Specifically designed to handle very long inputs and maintain understanding across extensive text (documents, books, or long conversations).
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Additional Model Information

### Release Date
A date code in the model name (like 20240229) shows when the model was created or released, indicating how up-to-date its knowledge is.

### Sub-version
Smaller updates that make minor improvements without changing the main structure. These appear as decimal points or point releases (like v0.1, v1.2) after the main version number and represent fine-tuning rather than major changes.

## How This Applies to You

Since you can override AI engines in your preferences, understanding these differences helps you make informed choices. As you become more familiar with AI, you'll develop preferences for certain models.

### Key Considerations

<Tip>
**Balance Performance and Speed**: Larger models provide better results but take longer to respond. Choose based on your specific needs.
</Tip>

<Tip>
**Check Knowledge Cutoff**: Newer models have more recent training data. Look for recent release dates if you need current information.
</Tip>

<Tip>
**Consider Context Length**: Long-context models can handle much longer inputs, perfect for analyzing documents or maintaining long conversations.
</Tip>

<Warning>
Some specialized models may have different safety filters or content policies. Always review model capabilities before use.
</Warning>
